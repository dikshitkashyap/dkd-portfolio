FUEL CARDS FALLBACK DESIGN UX PSYCHOLOGY

IOCL Generic Cards

Designing the fallback without making it the default

ROLE Sole Product Designer
PLATFORMS Android App + Ops Portal
OUTCOME 60% Increase in Card Issuances
01

The problem: A wall at every gate

Only 52% of interested users ever got an IOCL fuel card. The rest hit a wall somewhere in the verification chain and had no path forward.

23%
Dropped before portal entry — mobile verification and KYC failures
17%
Dropped at IOCL API rejection — fuel KYC issues and system rejections

The standard flow required CID creation, OTP verification, KYC checks, and IOCL portal dependency. Each step was a potential abandonment point.

02

The solution: Generic cards as a true fallback

Instead of requiring a card under the truck owner's identity, generic cards bypass CID creation entirely. The card is issued under BlackBuck's CID — no mobile verification, no KYC check, no IOCL dependency. The card works immediately for fuel transactions.

"How do you give users a shortcut without encouraging them to take it?"

A generic card is strictly a fallback for users who've genuinely hit a wall. But here's the design challenge: if the fallback path is easy to find, both users and operators will reach for it by default. Not from dishonesty, but because systems shape behavior more than instructions do.

03

Two users, two different solutions

I was designing the same feature for two completely different contexts — a truck owner stuck in onboarding, and an ops agent processing dozens of applications under time pressure. The right answer for one was wrong for the other.

04

For the truck owner: Relief at the right moment

DECISION 1: TIMING
Make the fallback contextually relevant, not a shortcut
The generic card option is not shown at the beginning of the flow, or even at the OTP screen when it first loads. It only appears after the 60-second countdown expires — as an orange banner at the bottom of the screen, below the keyboard. By that point, the user has already waited, already tried, and is already frustrated. The fallback arrives as a solution to a real problem, not a shortcut that competes with the primary flow.

The difference between an "option" and a "rescue" matters. An option invites comparison and deliberation. A rescue arrives when you're already stuck and just needs to get you moving again.

05

For the ops agent: Friction as guidance

An ops agent is not a frustrated truck owner. An agent processes high volumes under speed pressure, with incentives tied to throughput. If the generic card path is as easy as the primary flow, it stops being a fallback and becomes the default.

DECISION 2: FRICTION
Make the responsible action natural, the risky action intentional
The generic card feature exists on the ops portal, but it costs something to use. Not in steps, but in deliberate friction: structured reasons for the exception, a clear mode switch, and a moment of declared accountability. This friction isn't punishment — it's a guide that makes the trustworthy action feel natural.

I've written a detailed piece about designing exception workflows: The Why and How Behind Designing Exception Roads in UI

06

The outcome: System design at scale

The generic card feature isn't technically complex. What made this interesting was the design question underneath: how do you build a responsible shortcut?

The answer was different for each surface:

For the truck owner: The shortcut earns its way in — it only appears after the primary path has genuinely failed.

For the ops agent: The shortcut exists but costs something to use — deliberate friction in the form of accountability.

"The responsible action should feel natural, and the risky action should feel intentional. That balance is what makes systems trustworthy at scale."

This project resulted in a 60% increase in IOCL card issuances without compromising the integrity of the primary flow.
